## Market Data

You have three data sources. Here is what the metrics mean:

- **Score**: A hybrid demand signal combining review volume, quality gaps, and market density. Higher = stronger proven demand in the area.
- **Share**: The percentage of total nearby establishments this category represents. Low share with high score = underserved demand (opportunity). High share = saturated.
- **Pain**: Competitor weakness based on review analysis. HIGH = nearby competitors have significant complaints or long waits (room to do better). MED = moderate friction. LOW = competitors are well-reviewed.

## Your Task

Think through which business types would succeed at this property. Consider these dimensions:

1. **Restaurant demand signals** — Which categories show high scores with low market share? Those represent underserved demand. Which have high pain levels, meaning nearby competitors are doing a poor job and a better operator could capture share?

2. **Retail demand signals** — Same logic. High score + low share = opportunity. High pain = weak competitors a new entrant could outperform.

3. **Foot traffic alignment** — Heavy weekday midday and PM traffic points to office workers and commuters. Strong weekend traffic supports leisure and dining concepts. Weak morning traffic limits breakfast-only concepts. Match business types to when people are actually walking by.

4. **Space and rent constraints** — Consider the square footage. Small spaces (under 1,000 sqft) rule out large-format concepts. High rent per sqft demands strong revenue density — the business needs high throughput or high average ticket. If square footage or rent is not provided, note that but don't let it block your analysis.

5. **Location context** — The address, city, and surrounding neighborhood character matter. A concept that thrives in a financial district may not fit a residential side street.

Reason thoroughly about each business type that appears in the restaurant or retail analysis data. Explain why each one is a strong, moderate, or weak fit. Reference specific numbers from the data — scores, shares, pain levels, traffic counts.

Do NOT produce numerical scores. Do NOT produce a ranked list with numbers. Your output should be a structured written analysis, organized by business type or theme, that a second step can use to assign scores.
